Purpose

This is an implementation guide, not presentation commentary

Use this page after the session to run the system in your own store. The companion Prompt Pack contains the full copy-ready AI prompts. This guide tells you which reports to export, which prompt to run, how to review the output, what action to take and what result to record.

The central principle
Your 1.5% helps create attention and commercial opportunity. The store-level system determines how effectively those opportunities become customers, broader relationships and repeat revenue.

What the system is designed to improve

Activate

Customer count

Prioritise recent enquiries and open estimates, then follow up with a useful sequence rather than a generic chase.

Monetise

Average order value

Identify legitimate related requirements from the customer’s history, scale, locations, people, vehicles and events.

Reactivate

Purchase frequency

Find customers whose behaviour has moved outside their own normal purchase rhythm, rather than using one arbitrary inactivity deadline.

What the system does not do

  • It does not guarantee doubled sales.
  • It does not replace judgement, relationship knowledge or commercial responsibility.
  • It does not authorise customer data to be uploaded to an unapproved AI service.
  • It does not automatically contact customers.
  • It does not recommend selling to a customer whose existing issue should be repaired first.

Before opening the AI tool

Prepare the right data

Good prompts cannot rescue poor source data. Take ten minutes to prepare the files before running any analysis.

Signarama reports

  • Sales by Customer Detail
  • Estimate Conversions
  • Enquiries CSV or Gorilla Dash export
  • Work in Progress Summary
  • Void Summary
  • Accounts Receivable Ageing
  • Relevant reviews or complaint records

Fully Promoted equivalents

  • Order history
  • Quote history
  • Customer records
  • Enquiry sources
  • Work in progress
  • Replenishment history
  • Product categories and descriptions

Data preparation checklist

  1. Choose a consistent analysis period. Use enough history to observe patterns. Two to three years is preferable where available.
  2. Check dates and values. Confirm transaction dates are real dates, not text strings, and sales values exclude obvious credits or voids where identifiable.
  3. Preserve raw names. Do not manually merge similar customer names unless you are confident they are the same entity. The prompt will flag likely duplicates for review.
  4. Remove unnecessary personal information. The analysis generally needs customer or business identifiers, transaction dates, values and product information. It rarely needs private personal details.
  5. Use an approved AI service. Treat customer data as commercially sensitive.
  6. Name the files clearly. For example: Store_Sales_By_Customer_2024-2026.xlsx.
Important: Revenue history is only a proxy for customer value if gross margin data is unavailable. Do not describe revenue as true customer lifetime value without appropriate margin and cost information.
Mandatory guardrail

Repair before you reach out

Run Prompt 7, Relationship Risk Screen, before using any Activation, Monetisation or Reactivation list. A customer with unresolved WIP, a remake, aged debt or a complaint may require service recovery rather than another sales message.

1. Fix first

Pause selling. Allocate an owner, resolve the issue and communicate clearly with the customer.

2. Proceed with caution

Contact may be appropriate, but the relationship context must shape the message and timing.

3. Clear to contact

No visible operational barrier to a useful sales or reactivation conversation.

A practical rule: A cheerful “we haven’t heard from you” email to a customer with an unresolved problem is not marketing automation. It is automated provocation.

Run this check against

  • aged WIP and overdue milestones
  • voided jobs, remakes and reprints
  • 90-day-plus receivables
  • complaints and negative reviews
  • promises or corrective work not yet completed
Stage one

Activate: turn opportunities into customers

Primary metric: customer count.

Business question: Which enquiry or estimate deserves the next action, and what action gives us the best chance of conversion?

Files to upload

  • Estimate Conversions
  • recent enquiries or Gorilla Dash export
  • Sales by Customer Detail
  • optional open-estimate report showing last contact date
  • Relationship Risk Screen inputs where available

Step-by-step

  1. Open the Activate section of the Prompt Pack. Run Prompt 1, Activation Opportunity Analysis.
  2. Review the ranked list. Confirm that open estimates are genuinely live, customer matches are correct and no known relationship issue has been missed.
  3. Select a manageable shortlist. Start with five opportunities rather than producing a heroic list that nobody works.
  4. Run Prompt 2. Generate a tailored follow-up sequence only for the approved opportunities.
  5. Personalise again. Add facts known by the salesperson that were not contained in the report.
  6. Contact the customer. Every touch should add information, reduce uncertainty, answer an objection or make the decision easier.
  7. Record the outcome. Track response, next action, estimate status, conversion and timing.

What a useful AI output should contain

  • ranked open opportunities
  • why each matters
  • estimate age and value
  • new or existing customer
  • historical customer value
  • missing information
  • likely objection to explore
  • recommended next action
  • confidence level
  • contact sequence

Human review questions

  • Is the enquiry or estimate still live?
  • Has the customer already been contacted outside the system?
  • Is the customer match correct?
  • Is there a service, WIP or account issue?
  • Does the proposed message reflect the actual relationship?
  • Does each follow-up touch add value?
Example: A $45,000 estimate from a new prospect that is 110 days old is not automatically the next call. A $16,000 estimate from a repeat customer, 12 days old, with three completed jobs and strong past conversion may be more recoverable. The ranking should consider value, age, relationship, previous conversion and effort, not value alone.

Measures of success

Contact rate, response rate, first-contact time, estimates progressed, conversion rate, revenue activated and reasons lost.

Stage two

Monetise: solve more of the customer’s genuine requirement

Primary metric: average order value.

Business question: Based on this customer’s actual history and current business context, what commercially logical requirement should be discussed next?

Define the term clearly: Monetisation does not mean extracting more money. It means identifying and solving more of the customer’s genuine problem.

Files to upload

  • Sales by Customer Detail or order history
  • product or job descriptions
  • estimate history
  • customer profile information where known
  • sister-brand capability information

Step-by-step

  1. Run Prompt 3, Monetisation Opportunity Analysis. Ask the AI to classify historical orders into practical product categories and distinguish observed patterns from inference.
  2. Review the category mapping. Correct ambiguous product classifications before trusting the recommendations.
  3. Select accounts with evidence. Prefer customers with a strong relationship, identifiable category gaps, growth signals or predictable requirements.
  4. Apply the Physical Brand Footprint. Consider premises, people, vehicles, events, safety, gifting, promotional activity and new locations.
  5. Run Prompt 4. Create an account-development conversation, not a generic cross-sell email.
  6. Lead with discovery. Ask a commercially useful question before recommending a solution.
  7. Record categories discussed and outcomes. This improves the next analysis cycle.

Human review questions

  • Is the recommendation supported by actual history or a clear business fact?
  • Is it a genuine need rather than a generic product suggestion?
  • Is the timing relevant?
  • Does the customer have an unresolved issue?
  • Would the sister brand improve the solution?
  • Is the discovery question more prominent than the proposed sale?
Example: A safety-services customer with 28 employees, six vehicles, two premises, three events and eight expected hires has only purchased one external sign. The purpose is not to pitch twelve products at once. It is to identify the next most useful conversation, supported by the customer’s real circumstances.

Measures of success

Average order value, categories per customer, account-development meetings, additional categories sold, sister-brand referrals, account growth and gross margin where available.

Stage three

Reactivate: contact the right customer at the right time

Primary metric: purchase frequency.

Business question: Which previously valuable customer has moved outside their own normal purchasing rhythm?

Files to upload

  • Sales by Customer Detail or order history with dates
  • product or order categories
  • seasonal or replenishment information where known
  • Relationship Risk Screen inputs

Step-by-step

  1. Run Prompt 5, Purchase-Pattern Reactivation Analysis. Use at least three purchases before relying on a customer-specific cadence.
  2. Separate customer types. Regular-repeat, seasonal, project-based and irregular buyers should not be treated the same way.
  3. Review the expected next purchase date. Confirm that the pattern makes commercial sense and is not distorted by one-off projects or duplicates.
  4. Apply the Repair gate. Remove customers with unresolved service or account issues from the campaign list.
  5. Approve a small priority list. Start with the best five reactivation opportunities.
  6. Run Prompt 6. Build a natural reactivation sequence based on a plausible current need, not on telling the customer that an algorithm has judged them overdue.
  7. Record the result. Track response, meeting, estimate, order, revenue and time to purchase.

How to interpret cadence

CustomerObserved behaviourInterpretationAction
Customer ABuys about every 90 days; last purchase 155 days agoOutside normal rhythmPrioritise for review and likely reactivation

Customer CThree large projects at irregular intervalsProject account, cadence unreliableManual account review
Remember: Dormancy is a departure from expected behaviour, not simply the oldest transaction date.

Human review questions

  • Are there at least three meaningful purchases?
  • Is the cadence regular enough to use?
  • Is the customer seasonal or project-based?
  • Are credits, voids and duplicates excluded?
  • Is there a relevant reason to contact them now?
  • Does the message sound natural rather than algorithmic?

Measures of success

Response rate, reactivation rate, meetings, estimates, revenue recovered, time to next purchase and subsequent purchase frequency.

The operating method

Upload. Rank. Review. Act.

1. Upload

Provide the relevant reports for one Growth Loop stage. Avoid uploading every file merely because it exists.

2. Rank

Use AI to identify priorities according to value, timing, confidence, relationship and risk.

3. Review

A human verifies names, data quality, relationship context, commercial logic and tone.

4. Act

Begin a useful conversation, record the result and create the next action.

Use this short instruction before any campaign prompt

Before drafting any customer communication, stop after producing the ranked analysis. I will review and approve the shortlist. Do not state assumptions as facts. Clearly distinguish observed data from inference, flag uncertain customer matches and exclude any account with a material unresolved service issue.

Do not skip the human review

The AI may correctly calculate a pattern while misunderstanding the relationship. It may not know that the customer changed ownership, a quote was verbally declined, a project was postponed, or an issue is being handled outside the system. The review stage is not administrative clutter. It is where commercial judgement enters the process.

Your first implementation cycle

The 15 for 1.5 Challenge

Over the next 15 working days, select five opportunities in each Growth Loop stage. Small, worked lists beat impressive spreadsheets that quietly gather dust.

5 to Activate

Recent enquiries or estimates where conversion or follow-up may be incomplete.

5 to Monetise

Existing customers with genuine undeveloped needs.

5 to Reactivate

Customers whose buying has slowed against their normal rhythm.

For every selected account, answer

  1. What opportunity did the brand or marketing help create?
  2. What value has been captured so far?
  3. What genuine need remains?
  4. What is the next useful conversation?
  5. Could the sister brand add value?
  6. Is there anything that must be repaired first?

Measure results and improve the next cycle

After the 15-day cycle, use Prompt 8, Campaign Measurement and Learning. Do not simply total campaign revenue and declare it incremental. A stronger comparison uses a prior period, matched accounts or a holdout group where practical.

Activate metrics

  • contact and response rate
  • estimates progressed
  • conversion rate
  • revenue activated

Monetise metrics

  • average order value
  • categories per customer
  • sister-brand referrals
  • account growth
  • gross margin where available

Reactivate metrics

  • reactivation rate
  • repeat purchases
  • revenue recovered

Questions for the learning review

  • Which selection factors most strongly predicted a response?
  • Which messages produced useful conversations?
  • Which customer segments should be deprioritised next time?
  • Where did the AI make an inference that the salesperson corrected?
  • Which service issues repeatedly blocked selling?
  • What data field should be captured more consistently?

Troubleshooting

Common mistakes and how to avoid them

Uploading everything at once

Run one stage at a time initially. It is easier to review a precise output than a sprawling “analyse my business” response.

Confusing revenue with profit

Average order value and historical revenue are useful, but margin and capacity determine economic quality.

Treating every old account as dormant

Seasonal and project accounts require different logic. Cadence must be credible.

Using generic outreach

The customer should receive a relevant reason to engage, not evidence that their name was found in a spreadsheet.

Over-trusting category recommendations

A frequent product pairing is a clue, not proof that a particular customer needs it.

Skipping Repair

Do not invite another purchase while the first problem remains unresolved.

Working too many accounts

Start with five in each stage. Completion beats volume.

Failing to log outcomes

Without outcomes, the next AI run cannot learn which priorities or messages worked.
